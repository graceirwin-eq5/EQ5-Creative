🚀 How to Deploy a Website — From Markdown to Live
① Write in Markdown

Content is written in .md (Markdown) files — a simple text format using symbols like # for headings and * for bullets. Claude can generate these files based on prompts.

→
② Push to Repository

Files are saved into a GitHub repository using Terminal commands. The repo stores all versions of the project and acts as the source of truth for the website's code and content.

→
③ Connect to Host

The repository is linked to a hosting platform (e.g. GitHub Pages, Vercel, or Netlify). The host watches the repo and automatically rebuilds the site whenever new files are pushed.

→
④ Site Goes Live

The Markdown is converted to HTML and published to a live URL. Any future updates pushed to the repository are automatically reflected on the website in seconds.

Ad Strategy

Branded vs Unbranded Ads

Unbranded

Ads that do not mention a drug or brand name. Focus on raising awareness of a disease, condition, or treatment category. Lighter regulatory burden — no ISI required — but still go through MLR review. Used early in the funnel to educate HCPs before a prescribing decision.

Examples
  • Disease awareness display ads ("Are your patients at risk for X?")
  • Condition education content on HCP portals
  • LinkedIn thought leadership on treatment gaps
  • Programmatic banners to a disease-state website
  • Dark social ads linking to patient screening tools
  • Sponsored webinars or CME content on a condition

Branded — No Claims

Ads that name the drug or brand but do not make specific efficacy or safety claims. Because no clinical claims are made, ISI is not required. Still subject to MLR review and must stay within FDA guidelines for reminder advertising.

Examples
  • Reminder ads showing only the brand name and logo
  • "Ask about [Drug Name]" awareness banners
  • Branded display ads with no indication or outcome stated
  • Sponsored content driving to a branded product website
  • Name recognition campaigns for newly launched drugs

Branded — With Claims

Ads that name the drug AND make efficacy or safety claims (e.g. survival rates, response data, dosing). These carry the highest regulatory burden — ISI is required, all claims must be annotated and referenced, and full fair balance must be maintained. Strictest MLR scrutiny.

Examples
  • Display banners with drug name, indication, and response rate
  • Email campaigns citing clinical trial outcomes
  • eDetail aids (CVAs) with dosing and efficacy data
  • Video ads referencing patient outcomes with scrolling ISI
  • Programmatic retargeting ads with branded efficacy messaging
Compliance Knowledge

MLR Submission PDF Components

🏛️

What is MLR?

MLR stands for Medical, Legal, and Regulatory — the three internal teams at a pharmaceutical company responsible for reviewing and approving all promotional materials before they go live.

  • Medical — ensures all clinical claims are accurate, supported by evidence, and scientifically sound
  • Legal — reviews for liability, copyright, trademark, and compliance with applicable laws
  • Regulatory — confirms the material meets FDA guidelines, includes required ISI/fair balance, and stays within the approved indication

No branded pharma ad can go live without passing MLR. The agency submits a PDF package with all required components for the team to review.

📋

Required PDF Components

  • Job Code (unique asset identifier)
  • Product Label / Indication
  • Annotated references for all claims
  • ISI (Important Safety Information)
  • PI (Prescribing Information) reference
  • Target audience / HCP segment
  • Channel / placement specifications
  • Off-label usage disclosures (if applicable)
  • Brand/legal disclaimers and fair balance
  • Submission date and version number
  • Creative dimensions and format specs
  • Copy matrix tied to annotated claims
  • Regulatory team contact information
  • Asset file names and SFTP confirmation

⚠️ Note: Confirm exact submission requirements with your regulatory team — requirements may vary by client and product.

Internship Reflection

Growth & Takeaways

Over the course of this internship, I grew most through doing — whether that meant building real campaign deliverables, learning Auto Layout through independent research, or developing a review checklist after catching my own mistakes. Collaborating remotely across cross-functional teams pushed me to become more resourceful, self-sufficient, and intentional in how I communicate and deliver work. Sitting in on client meetings, participating in 1:1s, and working on projects like the fake drug assignment and Mesoblast campaign gave me a ground-level understanding of pharmaceutical marketing that no classroom could replicate. I leave with stronger design fundamentals, a growing fluency in AI-assisted workflows, and a clearer sense of the kind of creative professional I want to become.
